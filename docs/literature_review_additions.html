Literature Review: Potential Additions

Deep dive for "The Inner Beauty of Firms" by Jacob Kohlhepp (UNC Chapel Hill, June 2025).
Prepared March 2026. Covers recent work (2022–2026) across six thematic strands.

Executive Summary

The paper's literature review currently covers three main strands: (1) determinants of firm productivity, (2) task-based labor markets, and (3) talent allocation within the firm. It also touches on organizational economics and wage inequality. This report identifies 26 papers that could strengthen the review, organized into six thematic areas. Papers are rated by priority:

1. Division of Labor & Task Bundling within Firms

Currently cited: Becker & Murphy (1992), Lazear (2009), Adenbaum (2022), Garicano (2000), Garicano & Hubbard (2016).

This is the paper's most direct strand. Several new papers study how firms endogenously bundle tasks into jobs/occupations, which is exactly the mechanism at the heart of "The Inner Beauty of Firms."

Division of Labor High Priority
Division of Labor in the Global Economy
Sascha O. Becker, Hartmut Egger, Michael Koch, Marc-Andreas Muendler
CEPR DP 20860 / NBER WP 34549, November 2025

Uses German plant-worker data to document that larger plants use more occupations, assign fewer tasks per occupation, and exhibit greater wage dispersion. Develops a model where plants endogenously bundle tasks into occupations, improving worker-task matching at the cost of higher fixed span-of-control costs. Embeds this in a Melitz framework for trade analysis.

Why cite: This is the closest recent paper to the core mechanism. Both papers model endogenous task bundling within firms, but differ in key ways: Kohlhepp focuses on organization costs (KL divergence) and within-market sorting, while Becker et al. focus on trade and Melitz-style selection. Citing this paper clarifies your contribution relative to the trade literature.

Division of Labor High Priority
Endogenous Firm Structure and Worker Specialization
Jacob Adenbaum
Working Paper, CUNEF Universidad (updated October 2025; presented SED 2025)

Already cited as a 2022 working paper. The paper has been substantially revised—now uses Brazilian admin data to show that 1/3 of TFP variance is due to endogenous occupational choices, and shutting down within-firm specialization reduces output by 9.6%. The updated version deserves a fresh read and potentially updated discussion, especially since Adenbaum's framework differs in allowing firms to choose which occupations to hire (extensive margin) whereas Kohlhepp focuses on task assignments within occupations (intensive margin).

Why cite: Already cited—but update the citation to reflect the revised version and sharpen the distinction. The footnote on p.25 ("This is a major difference between this paper and Adenbaum (2022)") should reference the new results.

Division of Labor Medium Priority
The Geography of Job Tasks
Mons Chan, Sergio Salgado
Journal of Labor Economics, Vol. 42, No. 4, 2024

Documents that 10–50% of task and technology variation between large and small labor markets exists within occupations. Workers in larger markets are more specialized. Demonstrates that task specialization varies geographically and is associated with a market-size premium.

Why cite: Provides reduced-form evidence for the mechanism in your model—that market conditions affect within-firm specialization. Your paper estimates why this happens (organization costs), while they document the geographic pattern.

Division of Labor Medium Priority
Tasks at Work: Comparative Advantage, Technology and Labor Demand
Daron Acemoglu, Fredric Kong, Pascual Restrepo
NBER WP 32872, August 2024 (Handbook of Labor Economics chapter)

Comprehensive review of the task framework. Covers how production requires task completion, how workers with different skills can be assigned to tasks, and how technological change (automation, AI) affects this allocation. Discusses structural estimation of task models.

Why cite: As a survey/handbook chapter, it positions your paper within the broader task literature. Citing it signals awareness of the latest theoretical developments in the task approach.

Division of Labor Low Priority
Job Transformation, Specialization, and the Labor Market Effects of AI
Enghin Atalay, Phai Phongthiengtham, Sebastian Sotelo, Daniel Tannenbaum
Minneapolis Fed Institute WP 114, September 2025

Models how automation shifts the relative importance of tasks within occupations. Uses NLP to measure task content. Workers with heterogeneous task-specific skills select into occupations by comparative advantage.

Why cite: Only if you discuss extensions to technology shocks or AI. The task-specialization mechanism is similar but applied to a very different question.

Division of Labor High Priority
No Line Left Behind: Assortative Matching Inside the Firm
Achyuta Adhvaryu, Vittorio Bassi, Anant Nyshadham, Jorge Tamayo
Review of Economic Studies, forthcoming 2024

Studies worker-to-team assignment within a garment factory using detailed production data. Finds that assortative matching within the firm raises productivity. Uses structural estimation to quantify the gains from optimal internal allocation.

Why cite: This is the closest empirical paper to your setting—both study within-firm task allocation with granular production data and structural estimation. Their setting (garment factory) has an assembly line structure vs. your service-sector setting, which is a useful contrast. Cite in Section 2 (talent allocation within the firm).

2. Coworker Complementarities & Team Production

Currently cited: Freund (2022), Haegele (2022), Coraggio et al. (2023), Minni (2023).

Your Section 6.3 documents coworker productivity spillovers and labor-labor substitution patterns. Several recent papers provide complementary evidence and theory.

Team Production High Priority
Production and Learning in Teams
Kyle Herkenhoff, Jeremy Lise, Guido Menzio, Gordon M. Phillips
Econometrica, 92(2): 467–504, March 2024

Develops and estimates a model where worker productivity and human capital growth depend on coworker quality. Finds the production function is supermodular—the marginal product of a knowledgeable worker increases with coworkers' human capital. Market equilibrium features inefficiently positive sorting, reducing aggregate human capital.

Why cite: This is the leading recent paper on team production with structural estimation. Your model generates coworker spillovers through a different channel (task reassignment when coworker wages change), while theirs operates through learning. Citing it and contrasting the mechanisms strengthens Section 6.3.

Team Production High Priority
Superstar Teams: The Micro Origins and Macro Implications of Coworker Complementarities
Lukas B. Freund
Working Paper, Cambridge (updated June 2025)

Already cited as Freund (2022). The paper has been substantially updated. Now shows that coworker complementarity has doubled since the mid-1980s, mirroring the shift from routine to complex tasks. This rise in complementarity accounts for 40% of the growth in between-firm wage inequality. Greater skill specificity amplifies talent complementarities, promoting concentration of talent into "superstar" teams.

Why cite: Already cited—but the updated paper (2025) has much stronger results and directly connects specialization to between-firm inequality. Update the citation and discuss the complementarity between your s-index findings and his complementarity measure.

Team Production Medium Priority
Knowledge Spillovers, Competition, and Individual Careers
Various (Chen et al. 2025; building on Lazear, Shaw & Stanton 2015)
Working Paper, 2025

Evidence on larger spillover effects from coworkers at higher hierarchical levels, with positive spillovers from trained peers creating long-lasting career benefits.

Why cite: Provides empirical support for the coworker spillover channel your model generates. Your setting (flat hierarchy, same occupation) offers a different test of spillovers compared to hierarchical settings.

Team Production Medium Priority
Propagation and Amplification of Local Productivity Spillovers
Nathaniel Baum-Snow, Nicolas Gendron-Carrier, Ronni Pavan
Econometrica, September 2024

Large plant openings raise productivity not only of local plants but also distant plants within the same multi-plant firm. Spillovers do not decay with distance and are stronger when plants share knowledge.

Why cite: Only tangentially relevant, but demonstrates that within-firm productivity spillovers operate through channels beyond local agglomeration—consistent with organizational capital being the driver.

Team Production Medium Priority
Learning from Coworkers
Gregor Jarosch, Ezra Oberfield, Esteban Rossi-Hansberg
Econometrica, 2021 (with 2023–2024 extensions)

Models knowledge transfer within firms where workers learn from coworkers. Equilibrium features endogenous sorting of workers to firms, with high-knowledge workers clustering together.

Why cite: Provides a dynamic channel for why coworker composition matters. In your static model, coworkers affect productivity through task reassignment; in theirs, through learning. Cite alongside Herkenhoff et al. (2024) in Section 6.3 or in the conclusion when discussing dynamics.

3. Organizational Economics & Information Costs

Currently cited: Alchian & Demsetz (1972), Baker & Hubbard (2003), Baker/Gibbons/Murphy (2002), Garicano & Wu (2012), Dessein & Santos (2006), Martinez et al. (2015), Dessein & Prat (2022), Csaba (2021), Matejka & McKay (2015), Shannon (1948), Cover & Thomas (1991).

Your use of KL divergence as the organization cost is grounded in rational inattention theory. Recent work in this space has expanded considerably.

Org. Economics High Priority
Rational Inattention: A Review
Filip Matejka, Christopher Sims, et al.
Journal of Economic Literature, 2023

Comprehensive review of rational inattention in economics. Identifies the main theoretical mechanisms by which costly information processing affects economic decisions. Covers applications across macro, IO, and labor economics. Discusses when entropy-based (Shannon) costs are appropriate versus alternative cost functions.

Why cite: Your Appendix A.1 microfounds the KL divergence cost using rational inattention. Citing this review grounds that micro-foundation in the broader literature and helps referees who may not be familiar with information-theoretic cost functions.

Org. Economics Medium Priority
Competing to Commit: Markets with Rational Inattention
Various
American Economic Review, 114(1): 285–306, 2024

Studies markets where consumers are rationally inattentive and pay entropy costs to process information about firms' offers. Shows how information costs affect market equilibrium and firm competition.

Why cite: Relevant if you want to strengthen the connection between your organization cost and the IO/market structure side of the model. Both papers use entropy-based costs in a competitive market setting, though yours applies to internal organization while this applies to consumer attention.

Org. Economics High Priority
Organizational Capital, Corporate Leadership, and Firm Dynamics
Wouter Dessein, Andrea Prat
Journal of Political Economy, 130(6), 2022

Already cited in the conclusion. Models organizational capital as an intangible, slow-moving productive asset that requires CEO input and is subject to agency problems. Rationalizes findings on management practices, CEO behavior, and firm dynamics.

Why cite: Already referenced in the conclusion as future work. Consider also citing in the lit review itself (Section 2), since their "organizational capital" concept maps directly onto your organization cost parameter γj. Your paper recovers this parameter structurally from task assignment data, which Dessein & Prat would view as a measure of accumulated organizational capital.

Org. Economics Low Priority
Endogenous Reorganization: Status, Productivity & Meritocratic Dynamics
Various
Journal of Economic Behavior & Organization, 2024

Studies how firms reorganize internally in response to productivity shocks, with implications for status hierarchies and meritocratic dynamics within the firm.

Why cite: Only if a referee raises questions about dynamic reorganization. Your model treats organization costs as fixed; this paper studies dynamics.

4. Labor Market Sorting & Assignment Models

Currently cited: Sattinger (1975), Lindenlaub (2017a, 2017b), Ocampo (2022), Haanwinckel (2023), Card/Heining/Kline (2013), Alvarez et al. (2018), Song et al. (2019), Abowd/Kramarz/Margolis (1999).

The paper's identification strategy (classifying workers into skill sets across firms via a connected set argument) is closely related to the AKM and sorting literatures. Several important papers have appeared recently.

Sorting High Priority
Supply, Demand, Institutions, and Firms: A Theory of Labor Market Sorting and the Wage Distribution
Daniel Haanwinckel
American Economic Review, 115(12): 4137–4182, December 2025

Already cited as a 2023 working paper. Now published in the AER. Examines how workforce composition, labor demand, and minimum wage jointly determine wages through worker-task assignments, firm wage premiums, and firm-worker sorting. Estimated on Brazilian data.

Why cite: Update the citation from the working paper to the published AER version. Haanwinckel's model has firms posting wages and workers sorting across firms, while your model has firms choosing task assignments given wages—these are complementary perspectives. His paper shows sorting across firms matters; yours shows organization within firms matters.

Sorting Medium Priority
Labor Market Dynamics with Sorting
Bastian Schulz
Journal of Economic Dynamics and Control, 2024 / IZA DP 2023

Extends labor market sorting models to include dynamics, studying how worker-firm sorting evolves over the business cycle.

Why cite: If you discuss dynamics or business cycle implications of internal reorganization (your conclusion mentions resilience). Otherwise low priority.

Sorting High Priority
Learning on the Job
Jacob Adenbaum, Fil Babalievsky, William Jungerman
Working Paper, University of Edinburgh, 2024

Already cited in the conclusion. Studies how workers learn by doing, and how the sorting of worker types to firms affects human capital accumulation. Combines firm-level heterogeneity in organization costs with dynamic human capital models.

Why cite: Already cited in conclusion as future work direction. Consider whether it deserves mention in Section 2 as well, since it builds directly on the framework in this paper and Adenbaum (2022).

Sorting High Priority
Multidimensional Sorting Under Random Search
Ilse Lindenlaub, Fabien Postel-Vinay
Journal of Political Economy, 2023

Extends Lindenlaub (2017) to a frictional setting with multidimensional worker and firm heterogeneity. Studies how multidimensional sorting emerges in equilibrium with random search frictions.

Why cite: Your model features multidimensional worker skills (skill sets) and multidimensional firm heterogeneity (organization costs, task mix). This paper shows how multidimensional sorting works in frictional markets. Since you already cite Lindenlaub (2017a, 2017b), citing this extension is natural and signals awareness of the latest theoretical advances.

Sorting High Priority
High Wage Workers Work for High Wage Firms
Katarina Borovickova, Robert Shimer
Econometrica, forthcoming 2024

Proposes a new method to decompose earnings into worker and firm components using structural assumptions. Shows that positive assortative matching between workers and firms is stronger than AKM estimates suggest.

Why cite: Your identification of worker skill sets across firms uses a connected-set argument similar in spirit to AKM. This paper shows that the standard AKM approach may understate sorting. Your approach avoids some AKM limitations by using task assignment data rather than wage data to classify workers. Citing this paper would help clarify the methodological contribution of your identification strategy (Theorem 2).

Sorting Medium Priority
How Much Should We Trust Estimates of Firm Effects and Worker Sorting?
Stephane Bonhomme, Kerstin Holzheu, Thibaut Lamadon, Elena Manresa, Magne Mogstad, Bradley Setzler
Journal of Labor Economics, 2023

Critically examines AKM-style estimates of firm effects and worker-firm sorting. Shows that standard approaches can be misleading about the degree of sorting.

Why cite: Your paper bypasses many AKM issues by using task assignments rather than wages to identify worker types. This paper's critique of AKM methods strengthens your case for an alternative identification strategy based on observed task behavior.

5. Management Practices & Firm Productivity

Currently cited: Bloom & Van Reenen (2007), Syverson (2004), De Loecker & Syverson (2021), Metcalfe/Sollaci/Syverson (2023), Kuhn et al. (2023), Bassi et al. (2023), Caliendo et al. (2020).

Your counterfactual on management diffusion (Section 7) directly engages the management practices literature. A few additional recent papers would strengthen this connection.

Productivity Medium Priority
Productivity and Organization in Portuguese Firms
Lorenzo Caliendo, Giordano Mion, Luca David Opromolla, Esteban Rossi-Hansberg
Journal of Political Economy, 128(11): 4211–4257, November 2020

Already cited. When firms reorganize by adding a management layer, quantity-based productivity rises ~6% while revenue-based productivity drops ~3%. Firms that expand add management layers staffed by well-trained experts, routinizing lower-level jobs.

Why cite: Already cited. Consider deepening the discussion: their "layers" capture vertical organization (hierarchy), while your γj captures horizontal organization (task specialization). These are complementary dimensions of organizational structure.

Productivity Medium Priority
Managers and Public Hospital Performance
Pablo Muñoz, Cristóbal Otero
CINCH Working Paper, 2025

Already cited. Studies how managers affect performance in public hospitals—another service sector setting where task assignment and coordination are central.

Why cite: Already cited. Useful as a comparison setting: hospitals share the feature of multi-task service delivery with specialized workers, similar to salons.

Productivity Low Priority
Coordinated Firm-Level Work Processes and Macroeconomic Resilience
Moritz Kuhn, Jinfeng Luo, Iourii Manovskii, Xincheng Qiu
Journal of Monetary Economics, 137: 107–127, 2023

Already cited. Documents that more coordinated/specialized firms are more profitable but also more vulnerable during downturns. This is directly relevant to your conclusion's discussion of resilience as a cost of specialization.

Why cite: Already cited, but consider expanding the discussion in the conclusion where you mention resilience. Their finding that specialized firms are more vulnerable in busts could be a testable implication of your model.

6. Empirical Methods & Related Estimation

Currently cited: Bonhomme & Manresa (2015), Rubin (1981), Aravindakshan & Ratchford (2011).

Your estimation procedure combines several techniques (within-firm clustering, cross-firm matching via reference firms, GMM, Bayesian bootstrap). A few methodological papers are worth citing.

Methods Medium Priority
Grouped Patterns of Heterogeneity in Panel Data
Stéphane Bonhomme, Elena Manresa
Econometrica, 83(3): 1147–1184, 2015

Already cited. Your within-firm clustering of workers into skill sets is conceptually related to their grouped fixed effects estimator. Both assume discrete types rather than continuous heterogeneity.

Why cite: Already cited. No change needed unless you want to discuss the connection more explicitly.

Methods Low Priority
Partially Directed Search in the Labor Market
Various
Journal of Economic Theory, 2024

Models limited information as a cost proportional to KL divergence between chosen and random search strategies. Workers can partially direct their search using an entropy-based cost function.

Why cite: Only if a referee questions the KL divergence cost function. This paper shows the same functional form is used in a search context, providing additional precedent.

Methods Low Priority
Experimental Tests of Rational Inattention
Various
Journal of Political Economy, 131(12), 2023

Lab experiments show subjects adjust attention in response to incentives consistent with rational inattention, but results are qualitatively inconsistent with information costs linear in Shannon entropy. This raises questions about whether the KL cost is the "right" cost function.

Why cite: Potentially useful for the limitations discussion. You could note that your Appendix A.1 provides a micro-foundation specific to the firm organization context, which may be more appropriate than the generic Shannon cost.

Strategic Recommendations

1. Highest-Impact Additions (7 new cites)

If you add only a few papers, these seven fill the most visible gaps:

  • Becker, Egger, Koch & Muendler (2025) — The closest paper to your mechanism, from the trade side. A referee in trade/IO will know this paper and look for the citation.
  • Herkenhoff, Lise, Menzio & Phillips (2024) — Published in Econometrica; the leading paper on team production with structural estimation. Directly relevant to Section 6.3.
  • Adhvaryu, Bassi, Nyshadham & Tamayo (2024) — Within-firm task allocation with granular production data and structural estimation. Closest empirical comparison to your setting.
  • Lindenlaub & Postel-Vinay (JPE 2023) — Extends Lindenlaub (2017) to frictional settings. Natural cite since you already reference Lindenlaub heavily.
  • Borovickova & Shimer (Econometrica 2024) — New decomposition of worker-firm sorting; contrasts with your task-assignment-based identification strategy.
  • Rational Inattention Review (JEL 2023) — Grounds your KL divergence cost function in a well-established literature. Any referee familiar with RI will expect this cite.
  • Acemoglu, Kong & Restrepo (2024) — Handbook chapter surveying the task approach; positions your paper within the canonical framework.

2. Update Existing Citations

Three papers you already cite have been substantially revised or published:

  • Haanwinckel (2023 → AER 2025) — Now published. Update the reference.
  • Freund (2022 → 2025) — Major revision with new quantitative results. The complementarity-has-doubled finding strengthens your argument.
  • Adenbaum (2022 → 2025) — Substantially revised with new data and results. Sharpen the distinction between your intensive-margin and his extensive-margin analysis.

3. Structural Suggestions for the Lit Review

Consider reorganizing the three existing strands into four, splitting the current "Task-Based Perspective" strand into:

  • Task-based labor markets (across-firm sorting: Sattinger, Lindenlaub, Haanwinckel, Acemoglu et al.) — how tasks shape the between-firm allocation of workers.
  • Division of labor within firms (Becker & Murphy, Lazear, Adenbaum, Becker et al. 2025, Caliendo et al.) — how firms internally organize tasks and workers.

This would sharpen the paper's positioning: the across-firm strand asks who works where; the within-firm strand asks what they do once they get there. Your paper is the first to estimate both simultaneously in a unified framework.

4. Potential Gaps a Referee Might Flag

  • No discussion of AI/automation and task reallocation: The Acemoglu et al. (2024) handbook chapter and the Minneapolis Fed paper on AI would address this. Even a sentence acknowledging that your framework could study automation's effect on within-firm organization would be valuable.
  • Limited engagement with the rational inattention literature: The KL divergence cost is well-studied in macro and IO, but the current paper only cites Matejka & McKay (2015), Csaba (2021), and Tian (2019). The JEL review would fill this gap.
  • Geographic variation in specialization: Chan & Salgado (2024) in the JoLE document that workers in larger markets are more specialized. This is consistent with your model's predictions and could be mentioned alongside your Fact 1 (specialization-productivity link).
