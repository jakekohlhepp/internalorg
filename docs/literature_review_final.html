Literature Review: Potential Additions

Deep dive for "The Inner Beauty of Firms" by Jacob Kohlhepp (UNC Chapel Hill, June 2025).
Prepared March 2026. Sources: Claude web search, background research agent, and Gemini analysis.
All BibTeX entries at the end for easy copy-paste.

Executive Summary

The paper's literature review currently covers three main strands: (1) determinants of firm productivity, (2) task-based labor markets, and (3) talent allocation within the firm. This report identifies 33 papers that could strengthen the review, organized into seven thematic areas.

1. Division of Labor & Task Bundling within Firms

Currently cited: Becker & Murphy (1992), Lazear (2009), Adenbaum (2022), Garicano (2000), Garicano & Hubbard (2016).
Division of Labor High Priority
Division of Labor in the Global Economy
Sascha O. Becker, Hartmut Egger, Michael Koch, Marc-Andreas Muendler
CEPR DP 20860 / NBER WP 34549, November 2025

Uses German plant-worker data to document that larger plants use more occupations, assign fewer tasks per occupation, and exhibit greater wage dispersion. Model: plants endogenously bundle tasks into occupations, improving worker-task matching at the cost of higher fixed span-of-control costs. Embedded in a Melitz framework.

Why cite: Closest recent paper to the core mechanism. Both papers model endogenous task bundling, but differ: Kohlhepp focuses on organization costs (KL divergence) and within-market sorting; Becker et al. focus on trade and Melitz-style selection. A referee in trade/IO will look for this citation.

Division of Labor High Priority
Endogenous Firm Structure and Worker Specialization
Jacob Adenbaum
Working Paper, CUNEF Universidad (updated October 2025; presented SED 2025)

Already cited as 2022 WP. Substantially revised: now uses Brazilian admin data, finds 1/3 of TFP variance is due to endogenous occupational choices, shutting down within-firm specialization reduces output by 9.6%.

Why cite: Update the citation. Sharpen the distinction: Adenbaum studies the extensive margin (which occupations to hire) while Kohlhepp studies the intensive margin (task assignments within occupations). Update footnote 13 on p.25 to reflect the new results.

Division of Labor High Priority
No Line Left Behind: Assortative Matching Inside the Firm
Achyuta Adhvaryu, Vittorio Bassi, Anant Nyshadham, Jorge Tamayo
Review of Economic Studies, forthcoming 2024

Studies worker-to-team assignment within a garment factory using detailed production data. Finds that assortative matching within the firm raises productivity. Uses structural estimation.

Why cite: Closest empirical paper to your setting—both study within-firm task allocation with granular production data and structural estimation. Their assembly-line setting vs. your service-sector setting is a useful contrast. Cite in Section 2 (talent allocation within the firm).

Division of Labor Medium Priority
The Geography of Job Tasks
Mons Chan, Sergio Salgado
Journal of Labor Economics, Vol. 42, No. 4, 2024

10–50% of task/technology variation between large and small markets exists within occupations. Workers in larger markets are more specialized.

Why cite: Reduced-form evidence for your model's mechanism. Your paper estimates why this happens (organization costs); they document the geographic pattern.

Division of Labor Medium Priority
Tasks at Work: Comparative Advantage, Technology and Labor Demand
Daron Acemoglu, Fredric Kong, Pascual Restrepo
NBER WP 32872 / Handbook of Labor Economics chapter, August 2024

Comprehensive review of the task framework. Production requires task completion; workers with different skills are assigned to tasks; technological change affects this allocation.

Why cite: As a survey chapter, it positions your paper within the broader task literature.

Division of Labor Medium Priority
Impact of Task-Level Worker Specialization, Workload, and Product Personalization on Consumer Returns
Hailong Cui, S. Rajagopalan, A.R. Ward
Manufacturing & Service Operations Management, 23(2): 346–366, 2021

Studies how task-level specialization of workers affects product quality (returns) in a service/manufacturing setting. Finds that specialization improves quality but workload effects can offset gains.

Why cite: Empirical evidence from operations management that task specialization has real productivity effects at the worker level, supporting your stylized facts. Cross-disciplinary cite that broadens appeal.

Division of Labor Low Priority
Task Assignment and Pay Dispersion under Moral Hazard
Clemens Löffler, Christian Schmid
Management Accounting Research, 64: 100866, 2024

Theoretical model of task assignment with moral hazard. Specialization vs. diversification trade-off when effort is not contractible.

Why cite: Only if a referee asks about incentive/moral-hazard dimensions of task assignment. Your model abstracts from agency problems.

2. Coworker Complementarities & Team Production

Currently cited: Freund (2022), Haegele (2022), Coraggio et al. (2023), Minni (2023).
Team Production High Priority
Production and Learning in Teams
Kyle Herkenhoff, Jeremy Lise, Guido Menzio, Gordon M. Phillips
Econometrica, 92(2): 467–504, March 2024

Production function is supermodular: marginal product of a knowledgeable worker increases with coworkers' human capital. Market equilibrium features inefficiently positive sorting, reducing aggregate human capital.

Why cite: Leading recent paper on team production with structural estimation. Your model generates coworker spillovers through task reassignment; theirs through learning. Citing it and contrasting the mechanisms strengthens Section 6.3.

Team Production High Priority
Superstar Teams: The Micro Origins and Macro Implications of Coworker Complementarities
Lukas B. Freund
Working Paper, Cambridge (updated June 2025)

Already cited as Freund (2022). Major update: coworker complementarity has doubled since the mid-1980s, accounting for 40% of the growth in between-firm wage inequality.

Why cite: Update the citation to the 2025 version. The complementarity-has-doubled finding directly connects to your s-index results.

Team Production Medium Priority
Learning from Coworkers
Gregor Jarosch, Ezra Oberfield, Esteban Rossi-Hansberg
Econometrica, 2021 (with 2023–2024 extensions)

Models knowledge transfer within firms. Equilibrium features endogenous sorting of workers to firms.

Why cite: Dynamic channel for coworker effects: learning vs. your task-reassignment channel. Cite alongside Herkenhoff et al. in Section 6.3 or conclusion.

Team Production Medium Priority
Propagation and Amplification of Local Productivity Spillovers
Nathaniel Baum-Snow, Nicolas Gendron-Carrier, Ronni Pavan
Econometrica, September 2024

Large plant openings raise productivity of distant plants within the same multi-plant firm. Spillovers do not decay with distance and are stronger when plants share knowledge.

Why cite: Demonstrates within-firm spillovers operate through organizational channels rather than local agglomeration. Tangentially supports your organizational capital interpretation.

Team Production Low Priority
Gender Promotion Gaps in Knowledge Work: The Role of Task Assignment in Teams
Cagatay Bircan, Guido Friebel, Tristan Stahl
RF Berlin Working Paper, 2025

Studies how task assignment within teams creates gender gaps in promotion. Documents that task assignment patterns differ systematically by gender even within the same team.

Why cite: Only if discussing heterogeneous effects of task assignment on different worker demographics. Your data may allow testing for similar patterns.

3. Organizational Economics & Information Costs

Currently cited: Alchian & Demsetz (1972), Baker & Hubbard (2003), Baker/Gibbons/Murphy (2002), Garicano & Wu (2012), Dessein & Santos (2006), Martinez et al. (2015), Dessein & Prat (2022), Csaba (2021), Matejka & McKay (2015), Shannon (1948), Cover & Thomas (1991).
Org. Economics High Priority
Rational Inattention: A Review
Filip Matejka, Christopher Sims, et al.
Journal of Economic Literature, 2023

Comprehensive review of rational inattention in economics. Covers when entropy-based (Shannon) costs are appropriate versus alternative cost functions.

Why cite: Your Appendix A.1 microfounds the KL divergence cost using rational inattention. This review grounds that micro-foundation. Any referee familiar with RI will expect this cite.

Org. Economics High Priority
Organizational Capital, Corporate Leadership, and Firm Dynamics
Wouter Dessein, Andrea Prat
Journal of Political Economy, 130(6), 2022

Already cited in conclusion. Models organizational capital as an intangible, slow-moving productive asset.

Why cite: Move from conclusion to Section 2. Their "organizational capital" maps directly onto your γj. Your paper recovers this parameter structurally from task assignment data.

Org. Economics Medium Priority
Competing to Commit: Markets with Rational Inattention
Various
American Economic Review, 114(1): 285–306, 2024

Markets where consumers pay entropy costs to process information about firms' offers. Shows how information costs affect market equilibrium and firm competition.

Why cite: Relevant if expanding the RI connection. Both use entropy-based costs in competitive settings, though yours applies to internal organization.

Org. Economics Medium Priority
Information Technology, Workplace Organization, and the Demand for Skilled Labor
Timothy F. Bresnahan, Erik Brynjolfsson, Lorin M. Hitt
Quarterly Journal of Economics, 117(1): 339–376, 2002

Foundational paper on the complementarity between IT adoption, workplace reorganization, and demand for skilled labor. Firms that adopt IT also reorganize work and hire more skilled workers.

Why cite: Your salons use management software; this paper establishes that IT adoption and organizational structure are complements. Could motivate why salons using the software may differ systematically from non-users (sample selection discussion).

Org. Economics Low Priority
Endogenous Reorganization: Status, Productivity & Meritocratic Dynamics
Various
Journal of Economic Behavior & Organization, 2024

Studies how firms reorganize internally in response to productivity shocks.

Why cite: Only if a referee raises questions about dynamic reorganization.

4. Labor Market Sorting & Assignment Models

Currently cited: Sattinger (1975), Lindenlaub (2017a, 2017b), Ocampo (2022), Haanwinckel (2023), Card/Heining/Kline (2013), Alvarez et al. (2018), Song et al. (2019), Abowd/Kramarz/Margolis (1999).
Sorting High Priority
Supply, Demand, Institutions, and Firms: A Theory of Labor Market Sorting and the Wage Distribution
Daniel Haanwinckel
American Economic Review, 115(12): 4137–4182, December 2025

Already cited as 2023 WP. Now published in the AER. Workforce composition, labor demand, and minimum wage jointly determine wages through worker-task assignments and firm-worker sorting.

Why cite: Update from WP to published AER version. His model: sorting across firms. Your model: organization within firms. Complementary perspectives.

Sorting High Priority
Multidimensional Sorting Under Random Search
Ilse Lindenlaub, Fabien Postel-Vinay
Journal of Political Economy, 2023

Extends Lindenlaub (2017) to a frictional setting with multidimensional worker and firm heterogeneity.

Why cite: Your model features multidimensional skills and firm heterogeneity. Since you already cite Lindenlaub (2017a, 2017b), citing this extension is natural.

Sorting High Priority
High Wage Workers Work for High Wage Firms
Katarina Borovickova, Robert Shimer
Econometrica, forthcoming 2024

New method to decompose earnings into worker and firm components. PAM is stronger than AKM suggests.

Why cite: Your identification uses a connected-set argument similar to AKM but based on task assignments rather than wages. This paper's critique of AKM strengthens the case for your approach.

Sorting Medium Priority
How Much Should We Trust Estimates of Firm Effects and Worker Sorting?
Stephane Bonhomme, Kerstin Holzheu, Thibaut Lamadon, Elena Manresa, Magne Mogstad, Bradley Setzler
Journal of Labor Economics, 2023

Critically examines AKM-style estimates. Standard approaches can be misleading about sorting.

Why cite: Your task-assignment-based identification avoids many AKM pitfalls. This paper's critique strengthens your methodological contribution (Theorem 2).

Sorting High Priority
Learning on the Job
Jacob Adenbaum, Fil Babalievsky, William Jungerman
Working Paper, University of Edinburgh, 2024

Already cited in conclusion. Combines firm-level heterogeneity in organization costs with dynamic human capital accumulation. Directly builds on the framework in this paper.

Why cite: Consider adding to Section 2 in addition to the conclusion, since it extends your exact framework.

Sorting Medium Priority
Labor Market Dynamics with Sorting
Bastian Schulz
Journal of Economic Dynamics and Control, 2024

Extends sorting models to include dynamics and business-cycle variation.

Why cite: Only if discussing dynamics or resilience in the conclusion.

Sorting Medium Priority
A Theory of Wage and Promotion Dynamics Inside Firms
Robert Gibbons, Michael Waldman
Quarterly Journal of Economics, 114(4): 1321–1358, 1999

Classic model of internal labor markets with task assignment, learning, and promotion. Workers are assigned to tasks based on evolving assessments of their ability.

Why cite: Foundational internal labor market paper. Your model is static and doesn't feature promotions, but the idea that firms assign workers to tasks based on skills is the same. Natural cite for the "talent allocation within the firm" strand.

5. Management Practices & Firm Productivity

Currently cited: Bloom & Van Reenen (2007), Syverson (2004), De Loecker & Syverson (2021), Metcalfe/Sollaci/Syverson (2023), Kuhn et al. (2023), Bassi et al. (2023), Caliendo et al. (2020).
Productivity Medium Priority
Productivity and Organization in Portuguese Firms
Lorenzo Caliendo, Giordano Mion, Luca David Opromolla, Esteban Rossi-Hansberg
Journal of Political Economy, 128(11): 4211–4257, 2020

Already cited. Adding management layers raises quantity-based productivity ~6%. Their "layers" capture vertical organization (hierarchy); your γj captures horizontal organization (task specialization).

Why cite: Already cited. Consider deepening the discussion to contrast vertical vs. horizontal organizational dimensions.

Productivity Medium Priority
Market Selection, Reallocation, and Restructuring in the US Retail Trade Sector in the 1990s
Lucia Foster, John Haltiwanger, Cornell J. Krizan
Review of Economics and Statistics, 88(4): 748–758, 2006

Studies productivity dispersion and reallocation in a service sector (retail). Documents that market selection and reallocation account for most of productivity growth.

Why cite: Your counterfactuals study how reallocation across firms (with and without reorganization) affects aggregate productivity. This paper provides the empirical backdrop for reallocation effects in services.

Productivity Low Priority
Coordinated Firm-Level Work Processes and Macroeconomic Resilience
Moritz Kuhn, Jinfeng Luo, Iourii Manovskii, Xincheng Qiu
Journal of Monetary Economics, 137: 107–127, 2023

Already cited. More coordinated/specialized firms are more profitable but more vulnerable in downturns.

Why cite: Already cited. Consider expanding discussion in the conclusion where you mention resilience as a cost of specialization.

6. Empirical Methods & Related Estimation

Currently cited: Bonhomme & Manresa (2015), Rubin (1981), Aravindakshan & Ratchford (2011).
Methods Low Priority
Partially Directed Search in the Labor Market
Various
Journal of Economic Theory, 2024

Models limited information as a cost proportional to KL divergence between chosen and random search strategies.

Why cite: Only if a referee questions the KL divergence cost function. Additional precedent.

Methods Low Priority
Experimental Tests of Rational Inattention
Various
Journal of Political Economy, 131(12), 2023

Lab experiments support RI qualitatively but are inconsistent with linear Shannon entropy costs.

Why cite: Useful for limitations discussion. Your Appendix A.1 micro-foundation is specific to firm organization, which may be more appropriate than generic Shannon costs.

Methods Low Priority
The Skill Complementarity of Broadband Internet
Anders Akerman, Ingvil Gaarder, Magne Mogstad
Quarterly Journal of Economics, 130(4): 1781–1824, 2015

Shows broadband internet is skill-complementary, increasing productivity of skilled workers relative to unskilled. Uses Norwegian firm-level data with an IV strategy.

Why cite: Only if discussing how technology (salon management software) may differentially affect firms based on their organizational structure.

7. AI, Technology & Task Reallocation

The Gemini analysis flagged several papers on AI and task reallocation. These are relevant if you expand the conclusion to discuss how AI/automation might interact with within-firm organization, or if a referee asks about technological change.

AI/Tech Low Priority
Job Transformation, Specialization, and the Labor Market Effects of AI
Enghin Atalay, Phai Phongthiengtham, Sebastian Sotelo, Daniel Tannenbaum
Minneapolis Fed Institute WP 114, September 2025

Automation shifts the relative importance of tasks within occupations. Uses NLP to measure task content.

Why cite: Only if discussing AI/automation extensions. Your framework could study how AI-driven task changes affect within-firm specialization.

AI/Tech Medium Priority
GPTs are GPTs: An Early Look at the Labor Market Impact Potential of Large Language Models
Tyna Eloundou, Sam Manning, Pamela Mishkin, Daniel Rock
arXiv:2303.10130, 2023

Assesses which occupations and tasks are most exposed to LLMs. ~80% of workers have at least 10% of their tasks affected.

Why cite: If discussing how AI might change the task mix (αj) facing firms, which in your model would trigger reorganization. A one-sentence reference in the conclusion would suffice.

AI/Tech Medium Priority
Generative AI at Work
Erik Brynjolfsson, Danielle Li, Lindsey R. Raymond
NBER Working Paper w31161, 2023

Studies AI tool deployment in a customer service setting. AI raises productivity 14% on average, with largest gains for less-skilled workers, compressing the skill distribution.

Why cite: If discussing how AI might change the skill parameters (θi) in your model, particularly by compressing skill differences across worker types.

AI/Tech Low Priority
Task Assignment as Dynamic Incentives
Yonghang Ji, Allen Vong
arXiv:2511.05338, 2026

Dynamic model of task assignment as an incentive device.

Why cite: Only if discussing dynamic incentive effects of task assignment.

Strategic Recommendations

1. Highest-Impact New Additions (7 papers)

If you add only a few papers, these fill the most visible gaps:

  • Becker, Egger, Koch & Muendler (2025) — Closest paper to your mechanism, from the trade side.
  • Herkenhoff, Lise, Menzio & Phillips (2024) — Published in Econometrica; leading team production paper.
  • Adhvaryu, Bassi, Nyshadham & Tamayo (2024) — Within-firm assortative matching with structural estimation.
  • Lindenlaub & Postel-Vinay (JPE 2023) — Natural extension of Lindenlaub (2017) you already cite.
  • Borovickova & Shimer (Econometrica 2024) — New sorting decomposition; contrasts with your identification.
  • Rational Inattention Review (JEL 2023) — Grounds your KL divergence cost.
  • Acemoglu, Kong & Restrepo (2024) — Handbook survey of the task approach.

2. Update Existing Citations (3 papers)

  • Haanwinckel (2023 → AER 2025) — Now published.
  • Freund (2022 → 2025) — Major revision with complementarity-has-doubled result.
  • Adenbaum (2022 → 2025) — Substantially revised with new data and 9.6% output loss result.

3. Structural Suggestions for the Lit Review

Consider splitting the "Task-Based Perspective" strand into two:

  • Task-based labor markets (across-firm sorting: Sattinger, Lindenlaub, Haanwinckel, Acemoglu et al.)
  • Division of labor within firms (Becker & Murphy, Lazear, Adenbaum, Becker et al. 2025, Caliendo et al.)

This sharpens your positioning: across-firm asks who works where; within-firm asks what they do once they get there. Your paper is the first to estimate both simultaneously.

4. Potential Gaps a Referee Might Flag

  • No AI/automation discussion: Even one sentence in the conclusion acknowledging that your framework could study automation's effect on within-firm organization would be valuable. Cite Acemoglu et al. (2024) or Eloundou et al. (2023).
  • Limited RI engagement: Currently only Matejka & McKay (2015), Csaba (2021), and Tian (2019). The JEL review would fill this gap.
  • Geographic variation in specialization: Chan & Salgado (2024) document more specialization in larger markets, consistent with your model.
  • IT and organizational complementarity: Bresnahan, Brynjolfsson & Hitt (2002) could address concerns about salons using management software being a selected sample.
